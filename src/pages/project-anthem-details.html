The Client

Anthem is a rapidly growing international content and services company focusing on music publishing, recorded music, production music and film and television services. Anthem’s client roster includes hundreds of artists, writers, and rights holders of hundreds of syndicated television shows. One of the critical services that Anthem provides their clients is to gather, process, and distribute music royalties across various media sources (e.g. YouTube, Spotify, Radio, CD Sales, etc). While commercial off the shelf tools exist for royalty processing, they do not scale to process the sheer amount of royalties Anthem was collecting on a monthly basis.

The Problem

Anthem was starting to frequently experience processing lags and data correctness issues in their in-bound royalties and spent considerable human effort to manually process and distribute their clients’ royalties in a timely fashion. The error-prone process that had been setup made it challenging for staff members and leadership to trust the reports and metrics that were being produced by their systems. Put simply, it was becoming intractable to continue to grow Anthem’s catalog of artists and rights holders in the existing systems. To address these concerns and enable sustainable growth, Anthem partnered with Quadratica to develop a greenfield Royalty Processing System capable of handling upwards of 100 million in-bound royalty payment records per month and to accurately account for royalty distribution, all while minimizing potentially error prone human input into the system.

High Level Workflow

As a rights holder, your work can generate royalties in various places, be they online platforms like YouTube and Spotify or in more traditional settings like radio, CD and Vinyl sales, and sampling by other artists. Artists and rights holders work with companies like Anthem to help them ensure they are receiving the correct amount of royalties based on all the modalities that their music can be consumed. From an industry perspective, facilitation of royalties and management of copyrights, various “societies” are setup across the world that collect information on where registered rights holders’ songs have been played or purchased. Platforms such as YouTube send impression and play information to the societies which in turn enables the societies to bill YouTube the appropriate amounts for plays of the artist’s materials. The societies in turn use the registered rights management company for a particular work and similarly send royalty “transaction” records and bulk payment to the rights management company. For a company like Anthem, these “transaction” records number in the hundreds of millions per month and each record can be for as little as a single cent per “spin” of a particular work. Anthem is responsible for unpacking the bulk payment from the societies and distributing the payments to individual artists and rights holders based on their contractual obligations whilst simultaneously validating the correctness of the records and payments that the societies have made. Traditionally this was done by importing the royalty records into a commercial off the shelf system and manually reviewing each transaction, matching it to a particular rights holder and approving each transaction manually and eventually assigning credits to each rights holders’ account in the Anthem accounting systems. Suffice to say that with the scale of Anthem’s operations, this became an intractable solution. A more automated workflow was necessary.

Matching Royalties to Rights Holders

The key insight enabling a more automated workflow is that matching royalties to rights holders is by far the most time consuming and error prone part of the workflow. Several factors conspire against automation in this step of the process:

  • A fair bit of identification errors play into the process. For example, an artist such as Lindi Ortega might be misidentified as Linda Ortega by the society sending the royalties simply due to a transcription error.

  • While universal identifiers similar to ISBN exist to correct the first issue, they are not universally applied in the industry.

  • Societies sometimes send royalty transactions to the incorrect rights processor based on the incorrect or outdated information that they maintain.

  • Identifying the rightsholder based on the royalty record can be a complex process in itself. A single track may have multiple royalty holders (e.g. lyricist, vocals, management, survivors, etc), sometimes conflicting ones based on the different geographies where the song may have been played.

Quadratica worked with Anthem to create a royalty processing system that successfully navigated this problem space and automated the majority of the workflow whilst simultaneously increasing the accuracy of the royalty processing and accounting systems.

Identification Mismatch

Quadratica worked with Anthem to identify a set of heuristics that their royalty processing team used to match the society’s transactions records with Anthem’s internal databases and implemented versions of these to automatically assign probabilistic match percentages to each royalty transaction record. The Anthem team would then use these match percentages to quickly approve or change matches as necessary. We exhaustively tested this process with the Anthem team to build trust in the automated match percentages and reduced the manual workload to about 0.1% of what it was prior to this algorithm going in, saving countless hours of manual labour in the process.

Incorrect Transactions from Societies

While Anthem had an existing workflow for dealing with incorrect transaction records from the societies, it was a mostly manual process involving spreadsheets and significant human intervention. Quadratica worked with Anthem to map out the existing process end to end and created a custom user interface within the royalty management software to manage this case. This custom software build resulted in significantly faster turnaround times for identification and correction of incorrect transactions in the Anthem system.

Identifying Rights Holders

An individual song can have a multitude of rights holders based on a variety of industry specific factors. For example, a single song may have rights holders for the lyrics, performing artist and producer. Each of those rights may be transferred to others based on contractual clauses or in the event of a rights holders death. These rights may be fractually owned based on the number of people who are part of the performing group, producing agents and so on. Additionally, the rights of a particular track can be sold to others in particular geographic regions for business related reasons. The multiplicity of rights holders and conditions is difficult to capture and keep updated and would produce incorrect royalty transfers from Anthem to their rights holders. Quadratica and Anthem worked to model out the problem space of rights holders and created user interfaces to easy capture and update this information along with concrete approval and review processes built on top to prevent incorrect data entry from personnel. Overall, this increased the accuracy of payments to rights holders and increased internal trust in the automated systems that was being built.

Building for Scale

Quadratica architected and built a royalty processing server capable of ingesting hundreds of transaction records from the societies and creating matching payment records to rights holders in Anthem’s system. This involved a patented multithreaded/multiprocess transaction processing engine linked to a high throughput OLTP database to store the transactions and a high performance OLAP database to do reporting and manual “slice and dice” operations on. Quadratica built these systems in AWS’ cloud for maximum performance and availability and tested all functionality in an automated fashion to create a system that was trusted by the Anthem team. All in all, the gross payments generated to rights holders by this system were validated to be accurate within 1/100 of a cent which was inline with Anthem’s overall contractual agreements with their rights holders. In addition, the build of this system exposed data accuracy issues in other parts of the downstream systems that were corrected which also increased the overall accounting accuracy of the Anthem rights management division.

Conclusion

Quadratica and Anthem set out on an ambitious plan to significantly increase the processing capabilities of their royalty management team by utilizing technology and automation wherever possible. Through careful analysis of the problem space and an expert understanding of the rights management system, we managed to simultaneously decrease the amount of manual work that Anthem was doing to process royalties and increase the sheer volume of royalties that could be processed by Anthem’s growing business.
